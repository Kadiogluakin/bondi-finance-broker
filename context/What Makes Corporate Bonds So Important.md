What Makes Corporate Bonds 
So Important? 
	Corporate bonds are a crucial financial instrument for achieving and maintaining a 
healthy financial environment in the economy. They not only allow firms access to much 
needed liquidity but also offer investors - of both retail and institutional kind - a relatively safe 
and stable investment option to deploy their capital.

	Companies can raise capital using different methods. These methods range/span from 
traditional methods like equity financing and debt financing (including issuing bonds) to more 
out of the box ways like crowdfunding. Amongst all these methods, raising capital by issuing 
corporate bonds can provide a range of advantages to both issuers and investors compared to 
other methods.  

What Are Bonds and How Do They Work? 
	A bond is a method of debt financing in which the issuer - a firm in the case of 
corporate bonds, sells debt securities to investors to get the capital it needs. Basically, it is a 
type of loan which is financed by a crowd of investors, instead of a single entity like a bank or 
any other private lender. It’s basically a formal contract to repay borrowed money with interest 
at fixed intervals. The interval in which the investors receive the interest payment they’re 
1
entitled to can be annual, semi-annual, quarterly, or even monthly. When a bond matures; i.e 
after the borrower pays the final interest payment it owes to the investors, it also pays back the 
original amount it borrowed back to the investors who hold the bond. 

	Since bonds can be traded in secondary markets, the investors, who receive the 
interest payment after the issuance, or the original investment when the bond reaches maturity, 
can be different from those who lent the money to the issuer when the bond was first issued.

What Are the Advantages of Issuing Bonds for Firms 
Compared to Other Financing Methods? 
	There are several benefits of issuing bonds to raise capital compared to other methods. 
Let’s first analyze the two main financing options available to firms.

Equity Financing  
	One option is called Equity Financing. It means the owner(s) of a company can decide 
to sell a portion of their equity to raise capital. What makes this desirable is that the company 
is not required to pay the capital it received back to the investors. Since the investors who buy 
equity become a shareholder of the company, they also accept the risk of the business failing, 
therefore losing money on their investment. 

	However, along with buying the risk of business failure, the new shareholders also buy a 
right to have a say in the decision making process. This means the founders cannot act make 
decisions as they wish for the business. In fact, corporate directors have a direct obligation to 
make decisions in the best interests of the shareholders in the US, which is called the 
shareholder primacy norm. Ultimately, funding may have to be used for dividends or stock 
2
buybacks instead of a shiny new idea that requires R&D expenses.  

	Another disadvantage of equity financing instead of debt financing is that it is financially 
more expensive for firms to raise capital this way. Since equity investment is inherently more 
risky, investors expect higher yields. The outcome of these factors is a high dilution of the 
original shareholders’ equity,  especially for distressed companies or start ups.

	Debt Financing is the method of raising capital via borrowing. When a firm borrows 
money to finance whatever its needs are, it pays the original amount it borrowed, back, plus 
the interest. Interest is what the lender makes profit from. There are various advantages of debt 
financing for both the companies that take on the debt and investors who lend the funds. Debt 
financing comes in many forms which can range from bank lending and private lending to 
issuing bonds. Bonds themselves are a substantially diverse class of instruments, which we’ll 
touch upon later.  

	Primarily, the reason why a company would choose to borrow money instead of selling 
stocks is to keep the shareholder structure intact, therefore maintaining its independence. The 
ability to keep its ownership structure intact can allow a company to make business decisions 
on a longer time horizon instead of working to satisfy short-term demands of shareholders.

	Another advantage of debt financing over equity financing is the tax implications it 
brings. In most jurisdictions, interest payments on borrowed money is tax-deductible for firms. 
This can help reduce the tax burden on the company. Meanwhile, potential dividend payments 
made to equity investors are not tax-deductible. 

Advantages of Issuing Bonds Compared to Other Types of 
Debt Financing for Firms 
	There are two essential ways for firms to borrow money: bank loans and bond issuance. 
While it is beneficial for firms to maintain good relationships with banks to have quick access to 
liquidity by taking new loans or using lines of credit, issuing bonds can allow businesses to 
access a broader lender base, lower their cost of borrowing,  take on debt on their own terms 
rather than the bank’s terms, and escape systemic risk in unstable financial environments.

	There are two ways of issuing bonds in the market. Either a private placement or a 
public offering can be utilized to sell debt. While there can be a substantial difference in the 
number of investors who lend the money between private placements and public offerings, it’s

from a consortium of banks in rare scenarios that involve huge sums. The spreading of debt

	When loaning money, banks tend to heavily scrutinize the borrower to ensure 
compliance with the loan terms. This reduces the borrower’s independence compared to 
bonds. For example, a loan’s terms could limit the rights of the borrower to make further 
acquisitions or borrow more funds from either the original lending bank or another party until 
the loan is fully paid. Meanwhile, when a company borrows money by issuing bonds, it does 
not have to adjust its operations according to the loan it took. As far as the interest payments 
are made to the lenders, the business maintains its operational and financial autonomy.

	In a low-interest rate environment, bonds allow the issuer to lock-in a low interest rate 
loan for the duration of the bond. this gives the issuer the advantage of having low interest rate 
obligations if the interest rates go up before the bond reaches maturity. Contrarily, bank loans 
are largely lent on a variable interest rate basis, requiring the borrower to renegotiate the terms 
of the bank in case of a change in the interest rate environment. Although this may work in 
favor of the borrower if the interest rates decrease within the duration of the loan, it still carries 
the burden of renegotiation and retains the risk of increased loan repayment expenses. While it 
is true that a bond can quickly become a disadvantageous debt obligation if interest rates go 
down after the bond is issued, as long as the bond is callable, the borrower retains the 
opportunity to repay the loan before the maturity date and refinance with a new bond issued at 
a lower rate.

	A significant disadvantage of loans compared to bonds is the inherent systemic risk 
they carry. By their nature, banks are interconnected with each other and transmit financial 
shocks into the market. Therefore, bank-based financial structures, including bank loans, are 
heavily dependent on the conditions of the financial system. While banks tend to be generous 
in lending during optimistic financial conditions, they’re quick to slow down the flow of credit 
into the market during market downturns. Intrinsically, banks must be hesitant to sell loans

How Do Corporate Bonds Benefit Investors? 
	Investors often overlook corporate bonds when creating their investment portfolios, 
influenced by financial experts who typically recommend minimizing bond exposure in growth-
oriented portfolios, especially for young investors far from retirement. However, corporate 
10
bonds, particularly those issued by emerging market firms, can generate yields above 10% 
annually while maintaining a relatively safe profile. Considering that the historic average 
annualized return of the S&P 500 stock index has been around 10%, owning an asset that 
generates free cash flow comparable to equity returns unlocks further investment 
opportunities.

	The priority of debt holders in the event of business failures highlights the benefits of 
bonds as an investment class. In the event of a default, debt holders have seniority over equity 
holders during the liquidation process, contributing to the safety profile of these assets. Several 
advantages make corporate bonds an essential addition to a portfolio. Diversification is crucial 
in maintaining a healthy investment portfolio, and corporate bonds bridge the gap between the 
high volatility of equities and the low returns of sovereign bonds. In a high-interest-rate 
environment, the fixed income provided by these instruments offers a stable cash flow due to 
credit spreads compared to sovereign debt. Additionally, corporate debt offers significantly 
higher yields compared to savings deposits.

	Corporate bonds, considered a conservative financial instrument, allow investors to 
hedge against the risks posed by stocks, especially growth-oriented ones, in a portfolio. 
Moreover, corporate debt comes in various forms. Non-investment grade bonds, also known 
as junk bonds, offer high yields and potentially high returns on the original investment at the 
bond's maturity date. This variety within corporate debt instruments provides investors with 
options to tailor their portfolios according to their risk tolerance and return expectations.
